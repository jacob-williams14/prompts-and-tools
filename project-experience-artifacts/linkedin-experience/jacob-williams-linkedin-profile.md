## About

Here's the thing I've learned after nearly four years of building software for clients who don't all
speak the same technical language: the hard part is rarely the code. It's figuring out what the
people on the other side actually need — and then building something that makes sense for them, not
just for the spec.

I'm a software engineer at Atomic Object, a custom software consultancy, where I've gotten to work
across healthcare, statewide court systems, global education platforms, and consumer mobile apps.
That range is the part I value most. Every few months I've had to ramp up in an unfamiliar domain,
ask the questions that feel too basic to ask, and ship something real on the other side of it. It
turns out adaptability matters more than any single framework.

Somewhere in there I grew from an individual contributor into a co-technical lead — owning
architecture decisions, mentoring teammates, and translating between engineers and stakeholders. I
didn't set out to "become a leader." I just kept gravitating toward the moments where someone needed
a calmer voice, a clearer plan, or a problem untangled a few steps before it became a fire.

Day to day, that looks like full-stack development across React Native, TypeScript, and a handful of
backend stacks (Laravel, .NET, Node); system and API design; CI/CD and release pipelines; and the
kind of cross-functional work where design, content, and engineering actually have to agree. What I
care about underneath all of it: building teams that build great software — not just the software.

If you're working on something where the problem isn't obvious yet, that's the work I want to be
doing.
